def MeanAndSDev(a):
    mean = sum(a)/len(a)
    squares = np.square(np.add(a,-mean))
    sDev = math.sqrt(sum(squares)/(len(squares)-1))
    return mean,sDev

mean,sDev = MeanAndSDev(od)
print("Observed Brightness Mean = " + str(mean))
print("Observed Brightness S. Deviation = " + str(sDev))

Observed Brightness Mean = 300.0744
Observed Brightness S. Deviation = 9.858477752592451

I took 10000 observations of how bright my machinery read the signal 200 as by associating it to random numbers from the Poisson background. After plotting these 10k observations as a histogram, you can easily see how it takes the shape of a Poisson of the exact same width as the background, only shifted 200 units to the right. You can easily confirm this with numbers as the observed mean is effectively the mean of the Poisson background 100 plus the signal 200, and the observed standard deviation is effectively the same as the background, being close to 10.

This histogram represents a distribution of what brightness the machinery would see if a signal of 200 came into it; literally speaking, if 10000 signals of 200 came into it.

b. The observed brightness is biased towards being brighter than the true signal brightness. You can see this in the histogram, as its mean is about 100 units higher than the true signal of 200 units. The observed brightness distribution is likely symmetric about its own mean however, as it still follows a Poisson shape, but the histogram of course has a few asymmetric flaws from random sampling.

def sumForPPF(s_list,s_range,s_pList):
    s_total = sum(s_list)
    s_diff = np.diff(s_list)[0]
    s_area = 0
    s_uI = 0
    for i in s_range:
        if s_pList is None: # Histogram-like data
            s_area = s_area + s_list[i] / s_total
        else: # PDF-like data
            s_area = s_area + s_diff * s_pList[i]
        if s_area >= sig1:
            s_uI = i
            break
    return s_uI

I replotted the same plot from before, but zoomed in. The red line is the reading the machine took, being 500 units. The black line is the mean of the distribution. The cyan lines are the 1 Sigma uncertainty positions of the distribution. The vertical orange lines are the 1 Sigma uncertainty positions of the Bayes Theorem pdf. Due to the non-continuous nature of the pdf, the uncertainty lines of the pdf can easily be off by 1. Because of this, I believe that the distribution uncertainty points are more accurate than the pdf uncertainty points, since the massive amount of samples in the distribution increases its sensitivity, whereas the pdf's sensitivity is limited to integers due to its Poisson nature. This can also be seen in the symmetry estimations I took.

e.

First, a definition for my "symmetry" numbers: $Symmetry = \frac{x_{right} - x_{mean}}{x_{mean} - x_{left}}$, where xright is the uncertainty position on the right, and xleft is the uncertainty position on the left. If the symmetry is greater than 1, the distribution is weighted more on the left, and if it's less than 1, it's weighted more on the right, and the closer it is to 1, the more symmetric the distribution is.

On a practical level, I'd consider this distribution pretty much symmetric, since its symmetry numbers are fairly close to 1. Technically speaking, it won't be symmetric even after infinity samples, since the background is based on a Poisson with μ = 100, and Poisson pdf's are naturally asymmetric (but they become more symmetric as μ increases).

One thing to note here is that the mean of the distribution of signals that gave the machine a reading near 500 is around 400, making the readings biased towards being greater than the actual signals by around 100 units. This makes sense since even with no signal, the background readings taken by the machine have a mean of 100 units, so it naturally reads a static extra brightness of 100 units from anything.
